\section{Introduction}
The popularity of blockchain systems has exposed their inherent scalability
limitations. In particular, Bitcoin transactions incur high latency
(approximately $1$h~\cite{bitcoin}) and low throughput (on average 7 transactions
per second~\cite{scaling}), properties that are inherent~\cite{scaling} and
fundamentally constrain their suitability for frequent or time-sensitive
payments.

To address these limitations, pairs of parties (the \emph{endpoints}) can lock
funds with a single on-chain transaction, thus establishing a \emph{payment channel},
and subsequently exchange any number of payments \emph{off-chain}, i.e., by simply exchanging
direct messages and without adding any transactions to the underlying
blockchain. The endpoints resort to the blockchain only to resolve disputes over
their payment channel. When it is no longer needed, closing a payment channel
involves posting $O(1)$ transactions on-chain. Typically, if the endpoints
collaborate closing is cheaper, but either party can unilaterally close in case
its \emph{counterparty} misbehaves.

Multiple overlapping payment channels can be combined to
form a payment channel network (PCN). See,
e.g.,~\cite{DBLP:conf/fc/GudgeonMRMG20} for a survey of PCNs. The prime PCN on
Bitcoin is the Lightning Network (LN)~\cite{lightning}.

A fundamental limitation of existing PCNs is that off-chain payments between
non-adjacent parties (i.e., parties that do not share a payment channel, a.k.a.
multi-hop payments) require the active participation of all
\emph{intermediaries} along
a path of channels. This design introduces latency, creates availability
dependencies, and leaks information about the payment path.

\emph{Virtual payment channels} have been proposed as a remedy to this problem.
By performing an one-time off-chain setup over a sequence of preexisting
channels, the two endpoints establish a virtual channel which can then
be used for direct payments between them with complexity -- in the optimistic case --
independent of the path length. Once the virtual channel is no longer needed, it
can be closed off-chain if the involved parties cooperate, or unilaterally
on-chain in case of disputes. Intermediaries only need to interact during setup
and cooperative closing, both of which can be done fully off-chain. Payments
only involve messages between the two endpoints, just like those over adjacent
parties in a PCN (cf.\ Table~\ref{table:pcn-vs-virtual}).

While conceptually appealing, constructing virtual channels over Bitcoin is
challenging. Bitcoin does not support Turing-complete smart contracts or
covenants~\cite{10.1007/978-3-319-70278-0_12} and thus severely restricts the
design space of off-chain protocols. Existing constructions either rely on
expressive smart contract platforms~\cite{perun,DBLP:conf/ccs/DziembowskiFH18},
impose fixed channel lifetimes~\cite{10.1007/978-3-030-65411-5_18}, require
excessive collateral~\cite{donner}, cannot be stacked in a recursive
manner~\cite{donner}, or can only combine two underlying
channels (i.e., is \emph{binary})~\cite{9519487}. See
Sec.~\ref{sec:short-related-work} for related work.

A virtual channel constructor can be thought of as an \emph{operator} over a
collection of payment channels that produces a new virtual channel. The
preexisting channels are called \emph{base} channels.
We identify three natural desiderata for the virtual channel.

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Recursive}. A recursive virtual channel constructor can operate over
base channels that themselves could be the result of previous applications of the
operator. This allows building
new virtual channels on top of pre-existing virtual channels, allowing the channel
structure to evolve dynamically.
\item \emph{Variadic}. A variadic virtual channel constructor can be built on
top of any number of base payment channels directly. This allows building a
virtual channel between endpoints with multiple intermediaries with a single
setup, contrary to a \emph{binary} constructor.
\item \emph{Symmetric}. A symmetric virtual channel constructor offers setup and
closing operations that are symmetric in terms of computation, network and storage cost between the two
endpoints or the intermediaries (but not necessarily a mix of both). Also, payments by the two
endpoints incur the same delay. Importantly, this ensures that no
party is worse-off or better-off after an application of the operator in terms
of accessing the basic channel functionality, providing simple incentives.
\end{itemize}

Binary channels are also problematic as successive applications
of a recursive binary virtual channel operator to connect distant endpoints
breaks symmetry. This is of
particular concern since most previous virtual channel constructors proposed are
binary~\cite{DBLP:conf/ccs/DziembowskiFH18,9519487,10.1007/978-3-030-65411-5_18}.
Therefore having variadic channels is even more important.

As already identified~\cite{DBLP:conf/ccs/DziembowskiFH18}, recursiveness is an
important design property, but has been achieved only for fixed lifetime
channels~\cite{10.1007/978-3-030-65411-5_18} and has been left as an open
question for indefinite lifetime channels on Bitcoin~\cite{9519487}. Recursive
channels allow moving off-chain coins flexibly, quickly, with minimal
interaction, and at a low cost, even when the blockchain is consistently congested.
Parties can open new recursive virtual channels on an as-needed basis.
Recursiveness allows the off-chain moving of coins to a new channel even if the
base channel parties are unresponsive. It also enables moving only part of the
channel coins to a new channel. In contrast, coins in a non-recursive virtual
channel~\cite{donner} must be first moved on-chain, which is slow and expensive.
The flexibility of recursiveness can inspire confidence in virtual channels,
prompting users to transfer more coins off-chain and reduce on-chain congestion.

A scenario that needs both the recursive and the variadic
properties is as follows (Fig.~\ref{figure:graph}): Initially Alice has a channel with
Bob, Bob one with Charlie and Charlie one with Dave. Alice opens a virtual
channel with Dave over the $3$ channels -- this needs the variadic property.
She later realises she has to pay Eve a few
times, who happens to have a channel with Dave. Alice interacts just with
Dave and Eve to move half her coins from her virtual channel with Dave to a
new one with Eve -- this needs the recursive property.

Prior work falls short of achieving all three properties simultaneously in the
Bitcoin setting. Existing approaches support only binary
composition~\cite{9519487}, are not recursive~\cite{donner}, or impose
substantial collateral overheads~\cite{donner}. As a result, recursive,
variadic, symmetric virtual channels with indefinite lifetime over Bitcoin have
remained an open problem.

In this work, we resolve this open problem by introducing Elmo (named after St.
Elmo's fire), the first Bitcoin-compatible virtual payment channel construction
that is recursive, variadic, and symmetric, while supporting channels of
indefinite lifetime. We build on Bitcoin as it is the first and most established
blockchain, with formal security guarantees~\cite{DBLP:conf/crypto/GarayKL17}.

At a high level, Elmo enables two endpoints to establish a virtual channel over
an arbitrary sequence of base channels -- each of which may itself be virtual --
using a one-time off-chain setup protocol. Thereafter, payments proceed exactly
as in a simple payment channel, with constant round complexity and without
involving intermediaries. Virtual channels can be closed cooperatively entirely
off-chain, or unilaterally on-chain by any participant, with at most two
on-chain transactions per party and without dependence on the number of
intermediaries.

A key technical challenge is avoiding the need to store an exponential number of
transactions and signatures as channels are composed
recursively. Elmo addresses this by leveraging the \texttt{ANYPREVOUT} signature
type~\cite{anyprevout}, a candidate for inclusion in the next Bitcoin update,
which allows commitment transactions to be reused across different funding
outputs. We conjecture that without such a mechanism, efficient recursive virtual
channels over Bitcoin are impossible. For more details on \texttt{ANYPREVOUT},
see Sec.~\ref{sec:high-level}.

In summary, we make the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item We present Elmo,
the first Bitcoin-compatible virtual payment channel construction that supports
recursive and variadic composition with indefinite lifetime and symmetric costs.
\item We provide a formal proof of security in the Universal Composition
framework and establish real-world balance security guarantees.
\item We implement a simulation framework and demonstrate that Elmo performs on
par with or better than prior work while enabling strictly more expressive
off-chain interactions.
\end{itemize}
